BEA Professional Excellence Initiative — Speaker Notes

BEA Negotiating Team Presentation • Confidential
Use alongside the slide deck • Print or keep on phone
Opening: Slides 1–3
Set the Tone Immediately

Open by naming what you are NOT doing. "We are not here to complain — we are here with a fully built solution. We have answers to every question the district will ask, because we've already thought of them."

Establish the No Half-Measures Principle Early

The BEA's biggest temptation is to spread money thin and call it a win. Name this directly. "A token raise that doesn't fix the structure is not a win — it is a delay. We are asking for one complete fix."

The Case: Slides 4–5
The "Classroom Teacher Test" — Your Strongest Union Argument

This is the slide that should make every rep in the room uncomfortable — in a good way. Pause after you say it. Let it sit. "If the district told a classroom teacher they had to work five extra weeks unpaid, there would be immediate union action. That is exactly what happens to coaches every postseason. The only difference is that coaches stopped fighting it."

Reinforce: This is not "going above and beyond." This is mandated by IHSA or the governing activity body. The coach cannot refuse. That is the legal and ethical line.

Key phrase to hit verbatim: "Extended time is not when a coach chooses to do something extra — it is when the governing body of that sport or activity requires it. That is the line. Everything within that mandate is compensable."

Part 1 — Stipend Index: Slides 7–11
Lead With the Logic, Not the Numbers

Don't open with percentages. Open with: "Every time base salary goes up and stipends stay flat, coaches fall further behind. We are asking to link them — permanently — so this never happens again."

The "Not a Raise — A Correction" Line

If anyone pushes back on the immediate 5.34% bump, use this: "This is not a raise. The base salary grew for four years and stipends didn't. We are simply aligning them. The dollar amounts are modest — we can show you exactly what a Cat. D sponsor gains in Year 1: $149."

Objections on the Stipend Index — Quick Responses
  • "Budget creep" → "A percentage index is MORE predictable — same formula as base salary. You always know exactly what it costs."
  • "Takes from classroom teachers" → "Historically, stipends lose because they're in the same fight as salaries. Indexing removes that conflict — a salary win is now a stipend win too."
Part 2 — Extended Season Pay: Slides 12–17
The Ticket Taker Line — Use It

This lands every time. "At those same playoff games, the person selling tickets earns $25/hr. The coach who built the team, ran the practice, and is making real-time professional decisions on that sideline earns nothing extra. That is the contract we are asking to fix."

Not Just Sports — Bring In the Club Sponsors

Don't let this become a sports-only conversation. Reference the Math Team winning regionals and having to attend state. "That sponsor just had their season extended by two weeks — required by the governing body — for $0 in additional pay. This proposal covers them too."

The $5.28 vs. $45,658 Contrast — Say It Out Loud

When you hit the Pool of Money slide, read the numbers aloud and pause. "If the district took this $45,657 and spread it as a general raise to all 360 teachers, each teacher gets $5.28 per paycheck. Or — we make sure no coach or sponsor ever works a required postseason for free again. That is the choice."

Shared Interests: Slide 18
Lead With Agreement — Then Hold the Line

Entering negotiations as adversaries loses. Name what you share: "We want the same things — competitive programs, fair pay for time worked, fiscal responsibility. This proposal serves all three of those goals. We are not asking for something unreasonable. We are asking for alignment with values you already hold."

Retention angle: "When a veteran coach retires and we're not hiring a new teacher, who picks up that stipend? The qualified candidates in this district are already stretched thin. We have to retain the people we have — and compensation is how you do that."

Objections & Answers: Slides 19–20
BEA: "We have bigger priorities this contract."
The index rides alongside base salary — it doesn't compete with it. Extended Season is a separate modest line. These don't take from other priorities.
BEA: "Let's get something for coaches and come back to this."
"Coming back" has been the answer for multiple contracts. A token raise without indexing means the next contract starts from the same broken baseline. One complete fix now is better than three partial fixes over three cycles.
District: "We only have so much money — how do you want to use it?"
Use it to permanently fix the stipend structure and compensate required postseason time. Combined annual cost: less than 0.1% of budget. It removes this issue from every future negotiation.
District: "Automatic raises are unpredictable."
A percentage index is MORE predictable — same formula, same math as base salary. You already do this calculation. It doesn't change, it doesn't surprise you.
District: "We helped coaches last contract."
Stipends went up 1% at the start, then froze. Base salary increased 1.78%/year. That's falling further behind — not helping. A one-time flat bump without indexing guarantees this same conversation next contract.
Language Reminders
Say This
  • "Non-Guaranteed Time" (not playoff pay)
  • "Structural correction" (not retroactive raise)
  • "Required by the governing body" (not going above and beyond)
  • "Predictable, indexed compensation framework"
  • "Fiscal alignment with the Strategic Plan"
Not This
  • "We want more money" — too general
  • "Playoff pay" — implies sports only
  • "Retroactive raise" — triggers budget panic
  • "Budget increase" — sounds like a demand
  • "Coaches deserve..." — sounds emotional, not strategic
