A Solution Package, Not a Complaint

For Every Stipend Holder

This covers all stipend holders — sports coaches, club sponsors, fine arts directors. Not just athletics. This is a BPS101-wide issue.

"We want you to leave this meeting with a complete answer to every question the district will ask — because we've already thought of them."

No Half-Measures

The "Peanut Butter" Problem

When the district says "here is our pool of money — how do you want to use it?", the instinct is to spread it thin: a little for coaches, a little for other priorities, a little for something else.

Everyone feels heard. Nothing is actually fixed. The next contract starts from the exact same broken baseline.

What We Are Asking the BEA to Do

Resist the token raise. A small gesture that doesn't fix the underlying structure is not a win — it is a delay that gets harder to correct every contract cycle.

Fully fix one long-standing systemic problem. The annual cost is modest. The structural fix is permanent.

"Partially correcting three things and fully fixing nothing is not progress — it is the appearance of progress."

The Labor Rights Reality

The Classroom Teacher Test

Imagine the district told a classroom teacher: "Your contract ends in June — but you are required to keep working, unpaid, for up to five additional weeks."

There would be immediate union action. The BEA would never allow it.

What Coaches & Sponsors Actually Face

This is exactly what happens every postseason. The governing body requires it. The coach or sponsor cannot walk away. And it goes completely uncompensated.

This has been quietly accepted for so long that it has become invisible — even to the union that should be protecting these members.

"The only difference between this and forced unpaid overtime is that coaches stopped fighting it."

Required, Not Voluntary

Critical Distinction

Extended Time Is NOT

  • Watching extra film by choice
  • Running optional off-season workouts
  • "Going above and beyond" out of dedication
  • Any hour a coach or sponsor chooses to add independently

Extended Time IS

  • Time required because your program qualified for the next level
  • Mandated by the governing body — IHSA for sports, IESA or state-level bodies for activities
  • Work you cannot refuse without failing your students
  • Hours that exist precisely because of professional excellence

The Governing Body Standard — The Line in the Sand

If IHSA requires your team to compete, you must be there. If a state academic body requires attendance at the state championship, you must attend. That mandate is the trigger. All required work within it is professional, compulsory, and compensable.

Two Inequities. One Solution.

Problem 1 — Stipends Don't Grow

Stipends are fixed dollar amounts. When the district raises base salaries, coaches and sponsors get nothing. Every contract cycle restarts from the same frozen baseline — falling further behind each time.

Problem 2 — Qualifying Time Is Unpaid

Coaches and sponsors who lead their programs into postseason or qualifying competitions work weeks of extra required hours for $0 in additional pay — while event staff at those same competitions earn $25/hr.

"We should not financially penalize our most successful educators — asking them to work for a lower effective hourly rate, or no rate at all, the further their programs advance."
Part 1 of 2

The Stipend Index

Securing Automatic, Guaranteed Raises for All Stipend Holders

How the Index Works

Current Model

  • Stipends are flat dollar amounts
  • Base salary rises ~1.8%/yr — stipends stay frozen
  • Coaches must fight for increases every contract cycle
  • Pits stipend holders against classroom teachers for the same pool

Proposed Index Model

  • Each stipend becomes a % of the base salary
  • When base salary goes up, every stipend goes up automatically
  • No renegotiation needed — it is built into the contract structure
  • A win for base salary = a win for every coach & sponsor
"A win for base salaries is automatically a win for every stipend holder in the district."

Our Proposal

Index from the $50,000 Base (2023-24)
The "Natural Raise" Model

Set index percentages using the original 2023-24 base salary of $50,000.

Immediate effect: Applies an ~5.34% increase for 2026-27 — capturing four years of missed growth while the base salary increased and stipends did not.

Going forward: Every time the district raises the base salary, every stipend rises automatically by the same percentage. No renegotiation. Ever.

This is not a raise — it is a correction. Four years of professional work deserves to be valued the same way as four years of classroom work.

Anticipating the Pushback

Objection — "Budget Creep": Automatic escalators prevent accurate long-term budget forecasting.
Answer: A locked-in percentage improves forecasting — the district always knows exactly what stipends will cost because it is the same calculation as base salary. Dollar increases are modest: a head-level stipend gains roughly $200/year.
Objection — "Retroactive Jump": The ~5.34% immediate increase is an unbudgeted cash grab.
Answer: This is not a retroactive raise — it corrects four years of flat pay while the base salary grew. The dollar amounts are modest (a Cat. D sponsor gains ~$149 in Year 1), and the index creates fully predictable, forecastable costs from that point forward.
Objection — "Loss of Flexibility": Automatic raises drain money from classroom resources.
Answer: Historically, base salary wins because it affects more people — leaving coaches behind every cycle and creating internal conflict. Linking both eliminates the zero-sum fight. A win for teachers is a win for everyone.

Extended Season Pay

Non-Guaranteed Time. Professional Rate. All Programs.

The Ticket Taker Inequity

What Happens Today

Ticket sellers, announcers, timers, and chain workers earn $25/hr for game-day event support.

The coaches who lead those same teams into the postseason earn $0 in additional pay — their stipend covers only the regular season.

The Logical Problem

The contract defines the $25/hr rate for duties "which do not require the professional judgment of a licensed educator."

Postseason coaching — advanced strategy, direct student mentorship, high-stakes decisions — should never be paid at the same rate as selling tickets. It should be paid at the professional rate.

"We are classifying the most professional work in the building at the same rate as the most operational."

Not Just Sports — All Activities

The Same Logic Applies

A club sponsor's stipend covers the regular season — the normal schedule of meetings, rehearsals, and events.

When that program qualifies for a higher-level competition, the sponsor's required time extends — just like a coach whose team wins a bracket game.

Examples Across All Programs

  • Math Team — wins regional → required at state competition
  • Speech / Debate — IHSA sectionals → state advancement
  • Academic Decathlon — district → state qualifying
  • Robotics / STEM — regional → national qualifiers
  • Fine Arts — IHSA festival advancement rounds
"Extended time is not a reward for dedication — it is the direct consequence of excellence. We should compensate both the time and the excellence that caused it."

The Extended Season Pay Framework

The Rate

$35/hr

The contract's existing "professional rate" — already used for summer school, driver's ed, and curriculum work. Coaching and sponsoring at the postseason level qualifies by the same standard.

Overnight / State Trips

$420/day

12 hrs × $35/hr — recognizes coaches and sponsors are on duty around the clock for overnight and state trips. Per-diem replaces hourly tracking: clean, auditable, and defensible.

Accountability Built In

All hours pre-certified via a Playoff Calendar (e.g., "Standard Qualifying Week = 15 hours"). Triggered only by an official advancement. Approved by Head Coach or Sponsor + AD.

We use "Non-Guaranteed Time" — not "Playoff Pay" — because every sport's and activity's postseason structure is unique. This term applies equitably across all programs.

Billable vs. Non-Billable Time

Billable Time

  • Required practices beyond the regular season (~2 hrs/day)
  • Game or event day: setup + event + travel block (~6 hrs)
  • Supervising and coaching at qualifying competitions
  • Travel time to away or overnight state-level events

Non-Billable Time

  • Film review, game or event planning, prep work
  • Voluntary gatherings outside the approved schedule
  • Unapproved extra time added by the coach's own initiative
  • Any hours beyond the AD-approved maximum threshold

Sport- & Activity-Specific Rules Are Required

Example — Track & Field: All teams automatically advance to sectionals, so Non-Guaranteed Time does not begin there — only the state qualifiers' week counts.

Example — Math Team: Regular season meets are covered by stipend. Only the state competition week — triggered by winning a qualifying round — is compensable.

Closing the Argument

Shared Goals & Objections

Finding Common Ground — Then Holding the Line

What We Both Want

Competitive Programs

Both sides want BPS101 programs that attract talented students. That requires keeping talented coaches and sponsors — and competitive pay is how you do it.

Pay People for Their Time

Both sides believe employees should be paid for the time they work. Right now a coach's effective hourly rate drops to $0 the more successful they are — a direct contradiction of a shared core value.

Fiscal Responsibility

Both sides want sustainable, predictable solutions. This proposal is exactly that — indexed, capped, and built from 2-year historical actuals. Less than 0.1% of annual budget.

The Retention Problem We Share

As veteran coaches and sponsors retire, BPS101 is hiring fewer new teachers. The internal pool of qualified candidates is shrinking — and those who remain are already stretched thin. Competitive compensation keeps invested educators in these roles.

Strategic Plan Alignment

The district's own strategic plan calls for robust extracurricular opportunities. That goal cannot be fulfilled while actively disincentivizing the educators who run those programs. This is alignment with your stated priorities.

If the BEA Team Says…

Objection: "We have other priorities this contract — salaries, insurance, planning time. We can't give everything to coaches."
Answer: We are not asking the BEA to choose coaches over other priorities. The stipend index is a structural fix that costs the BEA nothing politically — it rides alongside whatever base salary increase you negotiate. Extended Season Pay is a separate, modest line that has been overlooked for decades. These do not compete with your other priorities; they complement them.
Objection: "Let's get coaches a little something and use the rest of the pool elsewhere — we can come back to this."
Answer: "Coming back to it" has been the answer for multiple contract cycles. A token raise without structural change means the next negotiation starts from the same broken baseline — and the problem compounds. We need one complete fix now, not another partial gesture that delays a real solution.
Objection: "This only benefits a small group of coaches and sponsors."
Answer: The stipend index benefits every single stipend holder in the district — every sport at every level, every club sponsor, every fine arts director. That is a significant portion of the BEA membership. And the labor rights principle — mandatory uncompensated time — protects every member.

If the District Says…

Objection: "We only have so much money for teachers — how would you like to allocate it?"
Answer: We'd like to use it to permanently fix the stipend structure and compensate required postseason time. The combined annual cost is less than 0.1% of the district budget — and it resolves a structural problem that has returned to the table every contract cycle. That is a win for both sides.
Objection: "Automatic raises tied to base salary create unpredictable budget exposure."
Answer: The opposite is true. A percentage index means the district can calculate stipend costs with the exact same formula they already use for base salary projections. It is more predictable, not less. There are no surprise negotiations, no special adjustments — just one consistent formula.
Objection: "We helped coaches last contract. We addressed it."
Answer: Stipends were raised 1% at the start of the last contract and then frozen — while base salaries increased 1.78% per year over the same period. That is not addressing it. That is falling further behind. A one-time flat bump without indexing guarantees this same conversation in the next contract.

Strategic Talking Points

End the "Success Tax"

Coaches and sponsors should not be financially penalized because their program wins. Advancement should increase professional value — not reduce the effective hourly rate to zero.

Internal Retention Is Cheaper

Fair compensation keeps our own teachers in coaching and sponsoring roles. External hires don't know our students, culture, or community. Retaining internal talent is the smarter investment.

Budgetary Certainty

The Pre-Set Calendar + Historical Average gives the district a defensible number. Less than 0.1% of budget, with zero risk of time-sheet inflation. This is the definition of fiscal responsibility.

Lead the Conference

Professionalizing the coaching and sponsor contract positions BHS as a leader in the Dukane Conference — attracting top-tier talent and setting the standard others will follow.
